Types of Dental Specialties: Periodontics

Welcome to the Types of Dental Specialties series! In this article, we will be focusing on Periodontics.

What does a periodontist do?

Periodontists examine a patient's gum health and play an important role in the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of gum disease (periodontitis). They can perform a variety of procedures, including the following:

  • Scaling and root planing: the gumline is deep-cleaned to remove tartar and bacteria, followed by antibiotic application
  • Gum graft: excess exposed tooth is covered using soft tissue from the patient's palate or a synthetic tissue to even out the gumline
  • Regenerative procedure: bacteria is removed and lost bone and tissue that were damaged from periodontal disease are restored
  • Laser treatment (LANAP): a laser is used to remove decayed tissues in the gums
  • Dental crown lengthening: excess gum and bone tissue is reshaped or removed to expose more of the teeth
  • Pocket reduction: bacteria is removed and pockets between supporting tissue and bone are re-secured into place
  • Dental implants: an artificial screw-like tooth (implant post) is placed into/on the jawbone and then covered with a crown once the implant post fuses with the bone and tissues
  • Tray delivery: a custom-fit tray is used to deliver medications prescribed by the periodontist

How much training does a periodontist need?

A periodontist requires 4 years of undergrad, 4 years of dental school, and 3 years of residency to start practicing. Tuition is normally required for all 3 levels of education and training.

What is a periodontist's salary?

On average, a periodontist earns $247,541 per year. This could be much lower or higher depending on the location and whether the periodontist owns their own practice. Practice owners will make more money than associates.
